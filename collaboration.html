Reflection on Collaboration

The first real decision our group had to make was which text to work with, and it took longer than we expected. We went back and forth between Sor Juana's La Respuesta and Montaigne's Essais for a while. Both texts are dense and rewarding in their own ways, but they pull in very different directions. Montaigne is endlessly interesting (arguable) and endlessly digressive, which is kind of his whole thing, but that quality made it difficult to imagine building a focused editorial argument around him. He resists the kind of pointed claim our apparatus needed to make. Sor Juana felt different as even in the opening pages, there is a direction to the text, a sense that she is going somewhere specific and that every rhetorical move she makes is in service of getting there. In the end, that thread we saw is what pulled us towards her.

How We Divided the Labor

Kristina is in the middle of golf season right now so we let her pick first and she went with the image program, which made sense given what she is already doing. From there, Tianna took the footnotes because she is honestly the most locked in out of all of us in class and we knew she would do it right. Steven ended up with the introduction which he was happy about because writing is sometimes his favorite thing to do in the moment, mostly because it is a break from the millions of calculus and chemistry problems he has waiting for him.

Where We Disagreed

Our biggest disagreement came around the editorial argument itself. Once we landed on the claim that Sor Juana was writing for future readers and not the Bishop, Tianna was not immediately convinced it was a strong enough position to build an entire apparatus around. Her concern was that it might be too obvious, that any careful reader of the text would already sense that something is off about the address, and that we would not be saying anything a smart reader had not already noticed on their own.

What changed things was thinking about what it actually takes to prove that argument from the text. It is one thing to feel that Sor Juana is not really talking to the Bishop. Showing exactly where and how the text gives that away, and explaining why a reader without the right historical and rhetorical context would miss it entirely, is a whole different thing. And that is where the apparatus actually lives. Once we got into that, Tianna was on board and we moved forward from there. (Sorry for throwing you under the bus Tianna)

How the Direction Was Decided

Honestly, it was not a formal decision. The argument came out of the same conversation where we were debating whether it was strong enough. The more we dug into the text together the more the future reader claim kept coming up naturally, and at some point it stopped being a theory we were testing and just became the thing we were all working from. Kristina's instinct toward the image program helped too, because thinking about what images would actually serve the apparatus forced us to ask what the apparatus was really arguing, and that question kept pointing back to the same place. From there the division of labor made sense and everything else followed.
