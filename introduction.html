Editor's Introduction

Steven Sanchez  ·  Literature Humanities, Spring 2026

Editorial Argument

La Respuesta is not a response to the Bishop of Puebla. It is a document written for future readers — and the opening pages are where that becomes most visible. Sor Juana uses the conventions of the humble reply letter as cover for a text she clearly intended to outlast the exchange that prompted it.

Why Read This Text

[Your introduction begins here. Address: why should a reader open this text? What is at stake in these five pages?]

In 1690, a sermon critique Sor Juana had written years earlier — never intended for publication — was circulated in print by the Bishop of Puebla, Manuel Fernández de Santa Cruz, under the pseudonym "Sor Filotea de la Cruz." Along with it came a letter from the Bishop gently rebuking her for spending her considerable intellectual energies on secular and theological controversy rather than devotional writing. La Respuesta — "The Response" — is her reply. It is sixty pages long.

Historical Moment

[Write about: New Spain, 1691. The position of a woman of letters inside a convent. The Inquisition as background pressure. The Bishop's power over her. What it meant to publish anything — and what it meant to let a private letter circulate.]

Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz (1648–1695) was the foremost intellectual figure of seventeenth-century New Spain and, arguably, of the entire Spanish-speaking world. She had entered the convent of San Jerónimo in Mexico City in 1669, not out of conventional piety, but — as she explains in La Respuesta itself — because convent life was the only available arrangement that would permit her to study. By 1690 she had accumulated a library of four thousand volumes, had corresponded with scholars across the Atlantic, and had published poetry, plays, and theological commentary. She had also accumulated enemies.

The Text and Its Formal Features

[Describe the genre: the familiar letter, the captatio benevolentiae, the rhetoric of humility. Describe what Sor Juana does with those conventions — and against them. Point to formal features a reader should track: the length, the citation density, the moments of direct address.]

"My sins, Venerable Lady, of ignorance have cast me into this presumption…"

The opening of La Respuesta is a captatio benevolentiae — a classical rhetorical move in which a writer, before saying anything else, positions herself as humble, unworthy, and grateful for the attention of her correspondent. It is a convention. Every formal letter of this period opens this way. What makes Sor Juana's opening remarkable is not that she uses the convention but what she does inside it: in the same breath that she disclaims her own ability to speak, she cites Saint Thomas Aquinas, Saint Paul, the Queen of Sheba, Moses, Saul, and Jeremiah. Real humility doesn't make that argument. Performance does.

Our Editorial Position

[State clearly what this edition argues and what it will emphasize. Why these five pages? What has this editorial team decided the reader needs to know — and what have you decided to leave out?]

The claim of this apparatus is that La Respuesta was written for posterity — that its stated addressee, the Bishop of Puebla, is a fiction maintained just barely enough to give the document its form, while its real audience is us. Several features of the text make this unavoidable. She writes far more than any act of contrition or compliance requires. She explains things the Bishop already knows — which only makes sense if she is writing for someone who doesn't. She signs her real name. She uses the conventions of submission to make arguments that are, on inspection, not submissive at all.

The notes in this apparatus are keyed to that argument. They mark the moments where her stated audience and her real one diverge. They gloss allusions not because allusions require glossing but because the density of Sor Juana's allusions is itself evidence — evidence that she is not writing for a bishop who already knows his Aquinas, but for a reader she is in the process of educating. The image program extends this argument into the visual domain: what did it look like to inhabit the position she inhabits? What does the space of her convent, the iconography of her tradition, the faces of women who wrote before her, tell us about what she was doing?

The passage we have selected — pages 153 to 157 in the Trueblood translation — is the opening movement of the letter, before Sor Juana turns to biography or direct argument. It is the most purely rhetorical stretch of the text, and therefore the most revealing. It is where she sets up everything that follows, and where the gap between her apparent and actual purpose is most visible to a careful reader.
