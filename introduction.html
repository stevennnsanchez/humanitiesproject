Editor's Introduction

Steven Sanchez  ·  Literature Humanities II  ·  Columbia University, Spring 2026

When you open La Respuesta, you are doing something Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz anticipated. She wrote sixty pages in response to a rebuke that needed, at most, two. She explained things to her addressee that her addressee already knew. She signed her own name when the man rebuking her had hidden behind a fake one. These are not the habits of someone writing a private letter of contrition, and the more time you spend with the opening pages, the harder it becomes to read them as anything other than a deliberate act of transmission to a future audience she could not name but clearly expected.

This edition argues that La Respuesta is addressed to future readers, and the opening pages are where Sor Juana builds the mechanism for sending it.

In 1690, Manuel Fernández de Santa Cruz, Bishop of Puebla, published without her permission Sor Juana's theological critique of a sermon by the Portuguese Jesuit Antonio Vieira and attached a letter of his own, signed under the female pseudonym "Sor Filotea de la Cruz," praising her intellect while firmly suggesting she redirect it toward devotion and away from controversy. He chose to hide behind a borrowed identity while exercising real institutional power over her, which is genuinely a choice that tells you something about what he expected in return. Sor Juana's reply, dated March 1, 1691, moves in the opposite direction entirely, writing at extraordinary length, signing her full name, and allowing the letter to circulate publicly, all of which are decisions you make when you expect your document to outlast its immediate recipient.

The selected passage runs from the opening address through her sustained deflections and into the moment she begins to assert, with great care, her right to speak at all. The rhetorical form she is working inside is the captatio benevolentiae, a classical convention in which a speaker opens by professing unworthiness in order to invite goodwill from the audience. In the very first paragraph, Sor Juana invokes Saint Thomas Aquinas, who remained silent before his teacher Albertus Magnus because he could find nothing to say worthy of Albertus (153), and then positions herself inside that same framework, claiming that she too knows "nothing worthy" of the Bishop. The comparison she is making is really extraordinary, since the Bishop becomes Albertus Magnus and Sor Juana becomes Thomas Aquinas, the Angelic Doctor of the Church and one of the most formidable theological minds in Christian history. Every concession she makes to the Bishop's authority creates space for the sentence that follows, which quietly exceeds what the concession seemed to permit.

She works this way throughout the passage. On page 154, she borrows from 1 Samuel to cast herself as a figure from the tribe of Benjamin, among the last among all the families of the least tribe of Israel (154), echoing Saul's words at the moment he was told he would be anointed king. Saul's protests of unworthiness preceded his elevation, and a reader steeped in scripture would have felt that echo immediately. The Bishop, a trained cleric, certainly would have. But the gloss she provides on the Samuel passage, the context she adds to make the reference legible, is written for whoever arrives at this text without that fluency, which is most of us now, and which she seemed to understand would eventually be most of her readers.

That is truly what makes the future-reader argument so strong. Throughout these pages, she keeps explaining things the Bishop already knew, glossing Quintilian, annotating her own biblical references, unpacking the Lusitanian Phoenix as a figure for Vieira (156) in a way that no seventeenth-century ecclesiastical reader would have needed unpacked. The only coherent addressee for those explanations is the reader who will arrive later, without the context, and need a guide through it. She is writing the guide while appearing to write a letter. Length alone really does support this, since a contrite reply to an ecclesiastical rebuke does not extend to sixty pages, and the excess cannot be explained by the demands of the genre or the conventions of the form. It can only be explained by an argument she was making to someone other than the Bishop.

The difficulty of being that future reader is real, and the notes in this edition exist to close that distance. The opening pages alone move through Aquinas, Albertus Magnus, Quintilian, Paul, Samuel, and the specific theological controversy over Vieira's sermon, often in the same paragraph, and none of it is backdrop. On page 156, Sor Juana writes that her silence will say nothing, because the silence will say nothing, which is a reworking of a mystical paradox rooted in 2 Corinthians 12, where Paul describes being carried to the third heaven and hearing things not granted to man to utter (156). Without that reference, the sentence reads like filler, when it's actually one of the letter's boldest moves. Sor Juana is claiming that what she knows outstrips what language can hold, and she's making that claim as a woman the Church was already punishing for knowing too much.

Some notes are definitional because the text demands it, and others trace the specific biblical and scholastic sources she draws on because her argument runs through them. A number engage directly with Grossman's translation, which is truly extraordinary, though there are moments where the English softens a rhetorical edge that is worth preserving, and this edition treats the translation as a literary achievement in its own right rather than a transparent window onto the original.

Sor Juana wrote La Respuesta knowing it would be read. The Bishop's intervention was already a public act, and her response was never going to stay private. She knew the letter would outlast its occasion, wrote past the Bishop to whoever came next, and left behind a trail of glosses and biblical parallels and rhetorical moves that only make sense if she expected readers who would need them. You are one of those readers. This apparatus is meant to help you follow where she led.
