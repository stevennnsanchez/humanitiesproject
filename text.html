Tianna Wang  ·  Literature Humanities II  ·  Columbia University, Spring 2026

Thesis: In the opening of Respuesta, Sor Juana uses different rhetorical strategies to satisfy the pressures of the church; however, she also guides a broader audience beneath those rhetorical strategies to interpret her layered message.

Very eminent lady, my senora:1Sor Juana formally addresses the letter to "Sor Filotea" — the Bishop's pseudonym — but this edition argues she wrote with a broader audience in mind.

Neither my will nor scant health nor reasonable apprehension has delayed my response for so many days.2The "delayed" response spans months: the Bishop's letter was from 1690; the Respuesta is dated March 1, 1691. "Reasonable apprehension" signals the real pressure of writing under church surveillance. Is it any surprise that my dull pen stumbled over two impossibilities at its first step. The first (and for me the more severe) is how to respond to your most learned, most prudent, most saintly, and most loving letter.3An example of captatio benevolentiae — exaggerated flattery that signals the humility is strategic rather than sincere. For if the Angelic Doctor of the Schools, Saint Thomas, when questioned regarding his silence in the presence of Albertus Magnus, his teacher, replied that he was silent because he could find nothing to say worthy of Albertus, with how much more reason should I be silent, not, like the saint, out of humility, but because in reality, I know nothing worthy of you.4Sor Juana compares herself to Thomas Aquinas and the Bishop to Albertus Magnus — a startling comparison delivered inside a profession of unworthiness. Appositive context suggests an audience beyond the Bishop. The second impossibility is how to thank you for the favor, as unwarranted as it was unexpected,5Her gratitude is rhetorically overextended — ten run-on phrases in one sentence — but "unwarranted" quietly signals the publication was not welcome. of having my rough scribblings printed; a good turn so immeasurable that it surpasses the most ambitious hope and most fantastic desire, which could find no place, even as a rational concept, in my thoughts; in short, it is of such magnitude that it not only cannot be reduced to the limits of words but exceeds the capacity of gratitude, as much for its dimensions as for how unforeseen it was, for as Quintilian said: Hopes give rise to the lesser glory, benefits to the greater. So much so that they silence the beneficiary.6Quintilian, author of Institutio Oratoria, was the foremost Roman authority on rhetoric. Sor Juana invokes him to legitimate her silence — aware that her own voice as a woman requires male authorization to stand.

When the happily barren, only to be made miraculously fruitful, mother of the Baptist saw so magnificent a visitor as the Mother of the Word in her house, her understanding became clouded and her speech failed, and so instead of thanks she burst into doubts and questions: Et unde hoc mihi7Luke 1:43 — Elizabeth's shock at Mary's visit. The Bishop would have needed no gloss; Sor Juana provides one anyway, pointing to a reader who does. From whence comes such a thing to me? The same occurred to Saul when he found himself elected and anointed king of Israel:81 Samuel 9:21 — Saul protests unworthiness just before being anointed king. Humility precedes elevation: the comparison is anything but modest. Am I not a son of Jemini of the least tribe of Israel, and my kindred the last among all the families of the tribe of Benjamin? Wherefore then hast thou spoken this word to me. And so say I: from whence, illustrious senora, from whence comes so great a favor to me? Am I by chance anything more than a poor nun, the most insignificant creature in the world9The captatio reaches theatrical excess. Since the Bishop already published her work as worthy, her insistence on unworthiness becomes visibly performative — a signal to the audience to read strategically. and the least worthy of your attention? Wherefore then speakest thou so to me? From whence comes such a thing to me?

To the first impossibility I can respond only that I am unworthy of your eyes, and to the second I cannot respond with anything other than exclamations, not thanks, saying that I am not capable of offering you even the smallest portion of the gratitude I owe you. It is not false modesty, but the candid truth of all my soul,10A crucial ambiguity: she insists her words are sincere while using rhetoric somewhat shamelessly. Sor Juana argues that performance and truth are not mutually exclusive. that when the letter your eminence called Atenagon reached me, I burst (although this does not come easily to me) into tears of confusion, because it seemed that your favor was nothing more than a reproach from God11A paradox: the Bishop's "favor" is recast as divine punishment. Her polite flattery continues, but describing the favor as a negative reaction hints that the Bishop's actions were not actually a favor. for how poorly I meet His expectations; while He corrects others with punishments, He wants to reduce me by means of benefits. A special favor, for which I know I am in His debt, as I am for other infinite benefits from His immense kindness, but also a special mode of shaming and confusing me: for it is a more exquisite method of punishment to have me, with my knowledge, serve as the judge who sentences and condemns my own ingratitude. When I ponder this, here in solitude, I often say: Lord may You be blessed, for You not only did not wish any other creature to judge me, and did not give me that responsibility either, but kept it for Yourself and freed me from me and the sentence I would have given myself — which, compelled by my own knowledge, could not be less than condemnation — and reserved that for Your mercy, because You love me more than I can love myself.12Sor Juana reclaims the punishment the Bishop imposed on her, redirecting guilt toward God. The sincerity of her religious feeling remains genuinely ambiguous.

Senora, forgive the digression that the power of truth demanded of me, and if I must confess the whole truth, this is also a search for havens to escape the difficulty of responding, and I almost decided to leave everything in silence, but since this is negative, although it explains a great deal with the emphasis on not explaining, it is necessary to add a brief explanation so that what one wishes the silence to say is understood; if not, the silence will say nothing, because that is its proper occupation: saying nothing. The sacred chosen vessel was carried away to the third heaven, and having seen the arcane secrets of God, the text says: He ... heard secret words, which it is not granted to man to utter.132 Corinthians 12:4 — Paul hears things in the third heaven he cannot speak aloud. Sor Juana parallels her constrained silence to Paul's, making a claim about the limits of language under external censure (see Note 2). It does not say what he saw but says that he cannot say; and so it is necessary at least to say that those things that cannot be said cannot be said, so it is understood that being silent does not mean having nothing to say, but that the great deal there is to say cannot be said in words. Saint John says that if all the miracles performed by Our Redeemer were to be written down, the entire world could not hold the books; and Vieira states that the Evangelist says more in just this passage than in everything else he wrote; the Lusitanian Phoenix speaks very well14The Lusitanian Phoenix is António Vieira — "Lusitanian" meaning Portuguese, "Phoenix" marking him as extraordinary. Sor Juana glosses the epithet for a reader who would not know it, while using a respected male voice to supplement her own. (but when does he not speak well, even when he does not speak well?), because here Saint John says everything he did not say and expressed what he did not express. And I, senora, will respond only that I do not know how to respond, will only give thanks, saying I am not capable of giving thanks to you, and will say, as a brief explanation of what I leave to silence, that only with the confidence of a favored woman and the benefits of an honored one can I dare speak to your excellency. If this is foolishness, forgive it, for it is a jewel of good fortune, and in it I will provide more material for your kindness, and you will give greater form to my gratitude.15Sor Juana hopes her writing will be given greater meaning in the future — a hint at a larger audience. Posthumously, a poem was found in her cell thanking supporters who thought of her "not as a nun struggling but as a great intellectual" (Project Vox).

Because he stammered, Moses did not think he was worthy to speak to the Pharaoh, and afterward, finding himself so favored by God fills him with so much courage that he not only speaks to God Himself but dares ask Him for impossibilities:16Exodus 33:18 — Moses moves from unworthiness to bold speech before God. This marks a shift in the text: Sor Juana is beginning to move from deflection toward assertion. Shew me thy face. And I too, senora, no longer think impossible what I wrote at the beginning, in view of how you favor me; because the person who had the letter printed without my knowledge, who gave it a title and paid for it,17Sor Juana documents the facts of publication — cost, title, lack of consent — in a tone that is still polite but increasingly confrontational. She is recording events for a later reader to understand what happened. who honored it so greatly (since it is entirely unworthy both for its own sake and the sake of its author), what will she not do, what will she not pardon,18Sor Juana uses feminine pronouns for the Bishop (maintaining Sor Filotea's identity), making the power dynamic ironic: how can another woman hold so much power when she does not? This portion feels most directly addressed to the Bishop specifically. what will she cease doing, and what will she cease pardoning? And so, under the assumption that I speak with the safe condition of your favors and under the protection of your kindness, and your having, like another Ahasuerus,19Book of Esther — Esther approaches the king at personal risk and is only permitted to speak if he extends the golden scepter. Sor Juana aligns herself with Esther, hinting to a larger audience that female speech is dangerous and must be granted by men in power. given me the tip of the golden scepter of your affection to kiss as a sign of granting me benevolent license to speak and propound in your illustrious presence, I say that I receive in my soul your most saintly admonition to turn my studies to sacred books, which although this letter comes in the guise of advice20Sor Juana teaches the reader how to interpret the Bishop's letter — not friendly advice, but a warning beneath a polite surface. This marks the transition from rhetorical deflection to direct argument. will have for me the substance of a precept, with the not insignificant consolation that even earlier it seems my obedience foresaw your pastoral suggestion, as well as your guidance, inferred from the subject and proofs of the same letter.

Endnotes

  1. 1
    "Very eminent lady, my senora:"
    A brief context: here, Sor Juana is responding to a letter from the Bishop of Puebla writing through the pen name of a woman, Sor Filotea de la Cruz. The Bishop published Sor Juana's critique of a sermon by priest António Vieyra (Carta Atenagórica) without her permission, and condemned her for not directing her writing toward more "useful" matters. Sor Juana formally addresses the letter to the "senora" (Sor Filotea); however, this edition argues she wrote her Respuesta with a broader audience in mind. Sor Juana's Respuesta was only published to the public posthumously in 1700, 5 years after her death. There is no physical evidence that there is an intended audience beyond the Bishop and yet textually seems to be written for more than just him. This apparatus will try to examine the nature of the intended audience and her rhetorical techniques through close reading.
  2. 2
    "Scant health and reasonable apprehension has delayed my response for so many days"
    The "delayed" response is referring to a span of a couple months: the Bishop of Puebla's letter to her was from 1690 and Sor Juana's Respuesta is dated to March 1st, 1691. When Sor Juana describes "reasonable apprehension" she alerts the reader to the context and real pressure she is writing in; Sor Juana is constrained in her writing because she is under the surveillance of the church. Already, she is providing reasoning for why reading between the lines is necessary; what she says throughout this Respuesta is often contradictory and not always what she means.
  3. 3
    "Most learned, most saintly, most loving"
    This is an example of captatio benevolentiae, a widely discussed aspect of Sor Juana's writing but still necessary to note. Captatio benevolentiae is a rhetoric used by Sor Juana to flatter the recipient and diminish herself in order to be more well-liked by the intended audience. In La Respuesta, its exaggerated form makes itself visible to the audience as a rhetoric, showing the audience that her humility is strategic rather than sincere.
  4. 4
    "in reality, I know nothing worthy of you"
    Sor Juana compares herself to Thomas Aquinas, a major theologian, and Sor Filotea to Albertus Magnus, his teacher and another major scholar. She attributes her silence thus far to the fact that she is unworthy in the presence of Sor Filotea's greatness. This is a continuation of captatio benevolentiae, but Sor Juana also uses appositive phrases in making the comparison. She writes as though she is giving significant contextual assistance that goes beyond what the Bishop would have needed, alluding to the idea that this piece is written for an intended audience that moves beyond the Bishop.
  5. 5
    "The second impossibility is how to thank you for the favor, as unwarranted as it was unexpected"
    Sor Juana expresses gratitude toward Sor Filotea to an excessive point. She breaks good literary form, writing ten run-on phrases within one sentence all to describe the extent of her gratitude. However, this is contrasted with the word "unwarranted," quietly signaling that the publication was not welcome on her end. Sor Juana's gratitude is rhetorically overextended in order to appeal to the Bishop but she also expresses discomfort to outside readers at the same time.
  6. 6
    "Quintilian said: Hopes give rise to the lesser glory, benefits to the greater. So much so that they silence the beneficiary."
    Sor Juana references Quintilian, a Roman rhetorician best known for his work Institutio Oratoria (The Orator's Education), a lengthy guide on rhetorical skills for public speakers. By citing Quintilian, Sor Juana backs her "silence" logically through an authoritative and respected figure so that her voice can be elevated. She is aware of her audience here and understands that her own voice as a woman is not enough to stand on its own. Instead, she must bring in other respected men.
  7. 7
    "Et unde hoc mihi"
    Sor Juana translates this as "From whence comes such a thing to me?," an allusion to the Gospel of Luke 1:43. She aligns herself with Elizabeth's shock and sense of unworthiness when seeing Mary (Mother of the Word). However, the Bishop would have understood this reference with little context. Instead, Sor Juana frames this scene for readers who may not recognize it immediately, pointing to an external audience.
  8. 8
    "The same occurred to Saul when he found himself elected and anointed king of Israel"
    Sor Juana alludes to 1 Samuel 9:21 in the Old Testament, where Saul protests that he comes from Benjamin, "the least" tribe of Israel, just before he is chosen as king. Here, she is aligning herself with Saul. On the outside, the reference reinforces her humility and lack of worth; however, the fact that Saul becomes a king immediately after shows to the audience that her humility has a purpose and can lead to future growth.
  9. 9
    "Am I by chance anything more than a poor nun, the most insignificant creature in the world"
    Her language extends the captatio benevolentiae to an almost theatrical extreme. Since the bishop has already acknowledged her writing as worthy of publication, her insistence on her unworthiness becomes more obviously performative. This sets the stage for her use of rhetorics strategically later within the letter, convincing an audience to read between the lines.
  10. 10
    "It is not false modesty, but the candid truth of all my soul"
    This is a crucial moment where Sor Juana's true intentions are ambiguous. She acknowledges the point made in endnote 9, that her modesty might read as performative; however, she stands her ground and insists that her words are entirely sincere. Despite using rhetorical techniques somewhat shamelessly, she wants to convince the audience to trust her. Here, Sor Juana expresses that use of rhetoric and the truth are not necessarily mutually exclusive. She needs to perform humility amidst pressures from the church to watch what she writes, but that doesn't take away from her wish to express herself honestly in the Respuesta.
  11. 11
    "your favor was nothing more than a reproach from God"
    Here, Sor Juana presents a paradox. She describes feeling punished through Sor Filotea's "favor" of publishing her critique. Though she writes it as a spiritual punishment that results from her own shame, the way the information is presented can also be read as a way to undermine the Bishop's actions. Her polite flattery thus far continues, but by describing the "favor" as something creating a negative reaction, she hints that the Bishop's actions were not actually a favor at all.
  12. 12
    "how poorly I meet His expectations… You love me more than I love myself"
    As a nun, Sor Juana presents her devotion to religion by glorifying God. However, the effect is not just pious. She is able to reclaim her own punishment and sense of guilt, which the Bishop took from her hands. This poses the question of the sincerity of Sor Juana's own religious guilt; does she feel it genuinely or has it been imposed on her externally?
  13. 13
    "He ... heard secret words, which it is not granted to man to utter."
    Here, Sor Juana alludes to Paul in 2 Corinthians 12:4. In the reference, Paul has heard information in the "third heaven" that he isn't allowed to speak out loud. Sor Juana parallels her situation to Paul's, telling the reader not to misread her silence as having nothing to say. Instead, she describes how silence can be a result of external constraints, a nod to the "reasonable apprehensions" she faced due to limitations that the church has imposed on her (see Endnote 2). Through this reference, she lets the audience know that her silence is meaningful.
  14. 14
    "the Lusitanian Phoenix speaks very well"
    The Lusitanian Phoenix refers to António Vieira, the Portuguese preacher whose sermon Sor Juana criticized in the Carta atenagórica. Lusitanian means Portuguese and Phoenix refers to a rare, extraordinary figure. She uses another respected figure to support her argument that silence does not mean she has nothing to say, and that meaning can be found in what she isn't explicitly saying. By using a credible and respected male figure, Sor Juana is again aware of her audience and uses others to supplement her own voice.
  15. 15
    "you will give greater form to my gratitude"
    In exchange for her gratitude and politeness, she writes that she hopes the Bishop will "give greater form" to her words. In this line, Sor Juana alludes to the idea that she hopes her writing will be taken with greater meaning in the future, hinting at a larger audience. Later, she succeeded in this goal; posthumously, an unfinished poem was found in her cell thanking her supporters for "breathing another spirit into [her]" by thinking of her "not as a nun struggling but as a great intellectual" (Project Vox, projectvox.org).
  16. 16
    "Moses did not think he was worthy to speak to the Pharaoh, and afterward, finding himself so favored by God fills him with so much courage that he not only speaks to God Himself but dares ask Him for impossibilities"
    Sor Juana references Exodus 33:18, paralleling herself to Moses, another character that feels unworthy in front of a figure of higher status. However, this comparison marks a change beginning in the text. Moses becomes bolder than how he started and Sor Juana exhibits a similar progression. At first, she described why responding to Sor Filotea felt impossible, but now, she feels that the attention which the Bishop gave her through the letter warrants her speaking up.
  17. 17
    "the person who had the letter printed without my knowledge, who gave it a title and paid for it"
    Sor Juana records the facts of the publication of her work, including the cost, title, and lack of consent involved. Though she preserves the appearance of politeness, her tone begins to feel more confrontational, documenting the circumstances for a later reader to understand that her writing was framed and published outside her control.
  18. 18
    "what will she not do, what will she not pardon…"
    Sor Juana chose feminine pronouns to describe the power of the person who published her work, aligning with Sor Filotea's fake identity. However, this makes the situation distinctly ironic; how can another woman hold so much power when Sor Juana does not? The answer, as we know, is that Sor Filotea is not actually a woman. However, she preserves his identity, instead asking for Sor Filotea to use "her" authority to pardon Sor Juana. Though the rest of the opening reads more like a speech to a larger audience, this portion seems specifically pointed at the Bishop.
  19. 19
    "like another Ahasuerus"
    Sor Juana references the Book of Esther here, where Esther approaches the king at personal risk. Esther is only allowed to live and speak if he extends the golden scepter, and Sor Juana aligns herself to this story. She reaches out to "Sor Filotea" to extend her kindness and allow her to continue writing, but also hints to a larger audience that female speech is dangerous and must be granted permission by men in power.
  20. 20
    "although this letter comes in the guise of advice…"
    In this line, Sor Juana is teaching the reader to interpret the Bishop's letter; she describes that it is not simply a piece of friendly advice but something more similar to a warning beneath a polite surface. This line marks a transition in the opening of the Respuesta; by calling his letter to her a "guise of advice," she uses phrasing that is clearly pointed and sharp rather than outwardly polite. This begins a shift to the body of her argument, moving away from the rhetoric that she used in the beginning toward her defense of learning.
